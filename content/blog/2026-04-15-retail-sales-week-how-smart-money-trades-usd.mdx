## The 48-Hour Smart Money Playbook for Retail Sales Week

### Step 1: Identify the Weekly Dealing Range Before the Release

The first thing I do — always, before any high or medium-impact news — is mark the weekly high and weekly low that printed in the sessions leading into the event. These aren't just technical levels. They're the pools of liquidity that institutional traders need to tap before they can reverse price or deliver a sustained move.

For USD pairs like EUR/USD, GBP/USD, or DXY-correlated instruments, I'm looking at:

- **Previous week's highs and lows** (external liquidity)
- **Monday and Tuesday highs/lows** if we're mid-week (internal liquidity building)
- **Equal highs or equal lows** on the 1H or 4H chart — these are magnets, not resistance/support in the traditional sense

If price has been grinding upward into equal highs above the current range before Retail Sales drops, that's a red flag. Smart money may be engineering a raid above those highs to collect buy-side liquidity before reversing hard on a weak print — or even a *strong* print if it suits their position.

### Step 2: Map the Institutional Order Blocks

Order blocks are the last significant down-candle before a strong bullish move (bullish OB) or the last up-candle before a bearish leg (bearish OB). They represent where smart money entered, and price will frequently return to these zones to pair remaining orders before continuing the intended move.

For Retail Sales week, I'm specifically looking at:

- **Daily order blocks** formed in the 2-3 sessions before the release
- **4H order blocks** that align with a premium or discount level on the weekly range
- Any **fair value gaps** (FVGs) sitting inside those order blocks — these are the highest-probability entry zones

I covered FVG entry confirmations in depth in my [ICT Fair Value Gap Trading Checklist](/trading-insights/2026-04-04-ict-fair-value-gap-trading-checklist-9-pre-trade-confirmations-that-separate-profitable-fvg-entries-from-losers) — that article gives you nine specific pre-trade filters before you ever click a button near one of these zones.

### Step 3: Watch the London Session Bias the Night Before

Session bias is one of the most underrated tools in the smart money concepts toolkit. London opens at 3:00 AM EST and is where the bulk of institutional forex volume is generated. The direction London takes in the 2-3 hours after open often telegraphs the true directional intent for the day — even if that direction initially looks like a fake-out to the untrained eye.

The night before a Retail Sales release, here's what I'm watching:

- Does London sweep the Asia session high or low first?
- Does it then deliver into a 4H order block in the opposing direction?
- Is there a clean FVG on the 15M or 5M chart that price entered and respected?

If London sweeps Asia lows, taps a bullish 4H OB, and then pushes into the New York open with bullish structure, that's a pre-positioning signal. Smart money has already shown their hand. Retail Sales data may then just be the *catalyst* for the continuation — or the excuse to shake out latecomers before the actual delivery.

### Step 4: The Release Itself — The Turtle Soup Moment

This is where retail traders get destroyed. The data prints. The candle spikes. Everyone jumps in the direction of the print.

But smart money needs your stop orders. So what typically happens?

1. News drops — price spikes in the "obvious" direction
2. That spike runs stops above/below the nearest swing high or low (the liquidity sweep)
3. Price reverses aggressively back through the news candle
4. Retail traders who chased the initial spike are now deeply offside
5. Price delivers in the *actual* intended direction, which may or may not match the data

This is sometimes called a Turtle Soup or liquidity raid, and it's a cornerstone of how ICT smart money concepts explain news-driven reversals. You can see the TradingView community's live analysis tools for tracking these sweeps in real time at [TradingView](https://www.tradingview.com) — I use it daily and have had multiple Editors' Pick features there.

The play isn't to trade the spike. The play is to **wait for the sweep, identify the displacement, and enter on the return into the FVG or order block that forms immediately after the raid**.

### Step 5: New York Session Confluence — Where the Trade Lives

The highest-quality setups I've seen in 10+ years of trading medium-impact USD data come not at the moment of release, but in the 30-90 minutes after the initial volatility clears. Specifically, the 10:00-11:00 AM EST window after a 8:30 AM release is where I've personally taken some of my cleanest trades.

By then:

- The liquidity raid has printed
- A displacement candle has formed (often a 5M or 15M candle that moves 20-40 pips with minimal wicks)
- A fair value gap has been created inside that displacement
- Price is pulling back to fill that FVG

This is where I'm entering, with my stop below the order block and my target at the opposing liquidity pool. Risk-to-reward on these setups typically ranges from 1:3 to 1:6 when the structure aligns cleanly.

## Common Mistakes I See Traders Make on Data Weeks

![A table displaying live trading positions for various instruments with P&L, leverage, and margin.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JidoLab/r2f-trading/master/public/chart-library/img-1776139798778-72nu.PNG)


I've mentored dozens of traders through setups exactly like this, and the mistakes are almost always the same. People don't lose money because the market is random — they lose it because they're operating on retail logic inside an institutional game.

The three biggest errors:

**1. Trading the initial spike without waiting for displacement confirmation.** This is pure gambling. The spike is designed to trap you.

**2. Ignoring session bias because the data surprised them.** A bearish session bias doesn't flip because Retail Sales beat. The institutional order flow is bigger than one number. Respect the structure that was already in place.

**3. Placing stops at obvious levels.** If your stop is at the last swing low, it's on smart money's shopping list. Push it below the order block, not above the nearest swing.

## Your Action Plan for This Week

Here's the short version of what to do right now:

1. **Pull up your USD pairs** (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, DXY) on the daily and 4H charts
2. **Mark the weekly dealing range** — external highs/lows, any equal highs or lows
3. **Identify the nearest 4H order blocks** above and below current price
4. **Note the FVGs inside those OBs** — these are your target entry zones
5. **Set alerts on those levels** so you're not watching a screen all day
6. **Watch London open** for the session bias signal
7. **After the release, let the dust settle for 30 minutes** before looking at entries
8. **Only enter if you have: swept liquidity + displacement + FVG pullback + session bias alignment**

Four conditions. All four. Not three. This is the discipline that separates the traders seeing consistent results from the ones blowing up on data days.
