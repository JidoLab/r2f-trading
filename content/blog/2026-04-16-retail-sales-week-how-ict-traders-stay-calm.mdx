## How ICT Trading Psychology Keeps You Out of Retail Sales Traps

Every time a medium-impact news event like Retail Sales hits the calendar, I watch the same thing happen in trading communities across the board. Traders who were disciplined all week suddenly start second-guessing themselves. Some freeze completely — they sit on their hands and miss legitimate setups that were right there in front of them. Others do the opposite: they overtrade, jump in ahead of the release chasing momentum, and blow through their risk parameters before the candle even closes. I've been doing **ICT trading** for over ten years, and I'll tell you plainly — Retail Sales week isn't a technical problem. It's a psychological one.

This article is my step-by-step mental framework for how I approach any Retail Sales release — and how I teach my students to do the same. Not with guesswork, not with gut feelings, but with a repeatable pre-news process grounded in liquidity context and smart money logic.

## Why Retail Sales Specifically Trips Traders Up

Retail Sales is considered a medium-impact economic release. It measures consumer spending across goods categories and gives the market a read on economic momentum. Unlike Non-Farm Payrolls or CPI, it doesn't always produce the explosive, directional moves traders crave — but it produces *enough* volatility to trigger emotional responses.

That middle ground is dangerous. If it were a high-impact release, most traders would know to step away entirely. If it were low-impact, they'd barely notice it. But Retail Sales sits in that uncomfortable grey zone where it *might* move the market significantly, and that uncertainty is exactly what causes poor decision-making.

You can read more about how economic data releases influence market structure in resources like the [CME Group's economic calendar education](https://www.cmegroup.com/education/courses/introduction-to-futures/economic-indicators.html) — but understanding the data itself is only half the battle. The other half is managing what the data does to *you*.

## Step 1 — Run Your Liquidity Audit Before the Week Even Starts

The first thing I do every week with a significant news event on the calendar is what I call a Liquidity Audit. Before I even look at potential entries, I need to understand where the liquidity pools are sitting on my key pairs.

Here's what I'm looking for:

- **Buy-side liquidity** resting above recent swing highs, equal highs, or trendline highs
- **Sell-side liquidity** sitting below swing lows, equal lows, or previous session lows
- **Unfilled Fair Value Gaps (FVGs)** from the prior week that price hasn't returned to
- **Inducement levels** — the obvious levels that retail traders are watching and where their stops are clustered

Once I know where liquidity is positioned, I can start asking the real question: *Is the Retail Sales release likely to be used as a catalyst to sweep one of these pools?*

This isn't about predicting the number. I don't care whether the headline beats or misses consensus. What I care about is whether the resulting price move looks engineered to take out a notable liquidity pool before reversing — which is classic smart money behavior around news events.

If you want a deeper breakdown of how to identify these patterns, my post on [how April NFP week liquidity patterns create hidden ICT entry opportunities](/trading-insights/2026-04-04-how-april-nfp-week-liquidity-patterns-create-hidden-ict-entry-opportunities-2026-market-update) covers a very similar process that applies directly to Retail Sales week.

## Step 2 — Define Your Participation Decision Before the Release

This step saves accounts. I call it the **Participation Decision**, and I make it the morning of the release — not in the heat of the moment when price is spiking.

I ask myself two questions:

**Question 1: Is there a high-probability setup already building in the pre-news session?**

If price has already swept a liquidity pool, returned into a premium or discount array, and is showing signs of displacement before the news hits — that's a setup worth watching. The news may just provide the fuel for a move that was already telegraphed by the structure.

**Question 2: Is the liquidity context ambiguous or unconvincing?**

If I can't clearly identify which side of liquidity is more likely to be targeted, or if price is sitting in a neutral range with pools on both sides at roughly equal distance — that's my signal to sit out. No setup. No trade. Full stop.

The mistake most traders make is that they make this decision *reactively* — they wait for the news to drop and then try to interpret it in real time. By then, emotion is running the show. Your pre-news Participation Decision should be cold, clinical, and made before the chaos begins. [Investopedia's breakdown of news trading pitfalls](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/trading/09/news-events-market-impact.asp) echoes this — the traders who get hurt most are those who react to the headline rather than the price structure.

## Step 3 — Set Your Three Mental States

Once I've made my Participation Decision, I lock in one of three mental states for the session. Think of these as modes your brain needs to operate in — and you need to choose the mode *before* the release, not during.

**Mode 1 — Observer Mode**
This is for when the liquidity context is ambiguous. You are watching only. No trades. No partial positions. No "just a small one." You are gathering data for the next session. This mode is underrated. Some of my best learning sessions have come from watching how price behaves around news from the sidelines.

**Mode 2 — Stalker Mode**
This is for when you've identified a probable setup but it hasn't triggered yet. You are waiting for specific conditions — a sweep of a key level, displacement into your array, confirmation through a lower timeframe entry model. You are calm because you know exactly what you're waiting for. You aren't watching every tick. You have a price alert set.

**Mode 3 — Execution Mode**
This only applies if a setup has already triggered and confirmed *before* the news release. You're already in a trade with a clear stop and target, and the news is just market noise. Your position is managed according to your original plan. You don't move your stop out of fear. You don't take early profit out of panic.

Most traders spend Retail Sales week cycling chaotically between all three without realizing it. Choosing your mode in advance is one of the simplest — and most powerful — acts of trading discipline you can develop.

## Step 4 — The 15-Minute Post-Release Rule

If you're in Stalker Mode and waiting for a post-release setup, here's a rule I've used for years: **Do not enter a trade within the first 15 minutes after the release.**

That initial 15-minute window is where the algorithm-driven volatility lives. Price is hunting stops, filling institutional orders, and creating the appearance of direction without committing to one. Retail traders who jump in during this window are essentially gambling on which way the sweep goes.

After 15 minutes, the picture starts to clear. You can see:
- Which side of liquidity was swept
- Whether price is respecting or rejecting key arrays
- Whether a displacement candle has created a new FVG worth targeting

Now you're trading structure, not noise. ## The Emotional Trap That Kills Accounts During News Week

I want to talk directly about the psychology here, because technical knowledge alone won't save you.

The emotional trap during medium-impact news weeks is what I call **FOMO-Fear Cycling**. It works like this: You're in Observer Mode, price moves sharply after the release, and you immediately feel like you missed something. That FOMO pushes you to enter a trade you never planned. That trade goes against you. Now fear kicks in — you hold too long hoping it reverses, or you cut too early and then watch it go your way without you. Either way, you feel worse than if you'd done nothing.

This cycle is responsible for more blown challenges and busted funded accounts than any single technical mistake. I've written about the most common errors that lead to this in [7 fatal mistakes that kill your funded account challenge success](/trading-insights/2026-04-03-7-fatal-mistakes-that-kill-your-funded-account-challenge-success) — and FOMO-Fear Cycling shows up in almost every one of them.

The antidote is pre-commitment. When you've already decided your mode, written down your conditions, and set your alerts — you've removed the in-the-moment decision-making that emotion exploits.
