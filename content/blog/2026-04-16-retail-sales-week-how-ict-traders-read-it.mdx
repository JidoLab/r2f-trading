## How ICT Trading Turns Retail Sales Week Into a Setup, Not a Surprise

Every time a medium-impact USD data release rolls around, I watch traders do one of two things. They either freeze up and sit on their hands, terrified of the spike, or they gamble — entering right before the number with a prayer and a tight stop that gets hunted before the real move even begins.

Retail Sales week is a perfect example. This report — tracked closely by the Federal Reserve and institutional desks alike — measures consumer spending across a broad range of goods. It's a pulse check on the U.S. economy. And yet most retail traders treat it like a coin flip.

ICT trading doesn't work that way.

In this breakdown, I'm going to walk you through exactly how I read the setup around a Retail Sales release: what I'm looking at before the number drops, how I'm using liquidity draws and order blocks to frame the trade, and how session timing changes everything. Whether you're trading Forex pairs, indices, or planning your next funded account attempt, this framework applies every single time a medium-impact USD event is on the calendar.

## Step 1: Mark the Draw on Liquidity Before the Week Opens

![Road_2_Funded leaderboard displaying a trader's 9th place, +80.24% profit, +$200k realized.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JidoLab/r2f-trading/master/public/chart-library/img-1776139791738-rqi6.jpeg)


The first thing I do — not on Tuesday morning, not when I open my platform — is mark the weekly draw on liquidity before the trading week begins.

Liquidity isn't random. Price is always moving toward a pool of resting orders: buy-side liquidity sitting above equal highs, sell-side liquidity resting below equal lows, or an untested fair value gap acting as a magnet. Before any economic data week, I need to know where price is *trying to go* on the higher timeframe.

For Retail Sales week, I start on the Weekly and Daily chart. Here's my checklist:

- **Identify the most recent swing high and swing low** on the Daily chart. Which one has more untouched liquidity sitting on the other side?
- **Mark any unmitigated order blocks** from the prior week — these are the last down-close candle before a strong bullish move, or the last up-close candle before a bearish displacement.
- **Note any open fair value gaps** on the 4H chart that haven't been rebalanced yet.

This tells me the *narrative*. Is price expanding to the upside to collect buy-side liquidity, or is it drawing down to sweep lows? The Retail Sales number will either confirm or temporarily disrupt that narrative — but the draw on liquidity usually wins.

## Step 2: Identify the Relevant Order Block Going Into the Data

Once I know the draw, I need a delivery mechanism — a place where smart money might step in to push price toward that draw.

For medium-impact USD data like Retail Sales, I'm primarily working on the **4H and 1H timeframes** for my order block identification, then dropping to the **15M** for refined entry.

Here's what a valid pre-news order block looks like in my playbook:

1. **Institutional candle print** — a single candle or small cluster that created the most recent displacement away from a key level. That candle's body (usually the last opposing candle before the leg) is your order block.
2. **It aligns with the premium or discount array** — I only buy order blocks in a discount (below the 50% of the range) and sell order blocks in a premium (above the 50%). Using the Fibonacci tool from the recent swing low to swing high gives me this instantly.
3. **There's an unmitigated fair value gap nearby** — a gap between the high of one candle and the low of a candle two positions ahead. These act as magnetic targets and also confirm where institutional interest lives.

The key rule for data week: **I do not enter into the order block before the data drops.** I wait. The news event is almost always used to manufacture a liquidity sweep first — a fake spike in one direction to grab stops — before the real move begins.

This is why I tell every student I mentor: the spike is the setup, not the signal. For more on this, my [CPI trading strategy breakdown](/trading-insights/2026-04-07-cpi-trading-strategy-how-smart-money-positions-24-hours-before-inflation-data-using-ict-order-blocks) walks through the same concept applied to inflation data — the positioning logic is nearly identical.

## Step 3: The Pre-News Consolidation Window (Asia and Early London)

Here's where session awareness becomes critical.

On the morning of a Retail Sales release (typically 8:30 AM ET), price behavior in the Asian session and early London session tells me a story. This is one of the most underused edges in ICT trading, and I consider it essential for navigating data weeks.

**Asian session (roughly 8 PM – midnight ET):**
This session typically builds the range. Price will often consolidate, forming equal highs and equal lows. I mark both. These become my liquidity pools. Which side gets swept first in London or New York is often a direct preview of where price intends to go after the data.

**Early London session (2 AM – 5 AM ET):**
Watch for a displacement — a sharp move that sweeps one side of the Asian range. If London sweeps Asian lows and then starts reclaiming upward, that's a bullish signature. If it sweeps highs and reverses, bearish. This London displacement frequently telegraphs the post-data direction, even before the number hits.

**The 8:30 ET window:**
When Retail Sales drops, price will almost always spike one direction first. This is the liquidity sweep I mentioned earlier. It grabs the stops that retail traders placed just beyond the pre-data range. After the sweep, I look for a return into my pre-marked order block on the 15M chart. That's my entry window — the 3 to 7 candles after the initial spike settles.

## Step 4: The Post-News Entry Framework

Let me make this as practical as possible. Here's my actual checklist for the post-news ICT entry:

**✅ Confirmation 1 — Market Structure Shift (MSS):** After the data spike, I need to see a break of structure on the 15M or 5M chart in the direction of the intended draw. A single impulsive candle closing above a recent swing high (for longs) or below a recent swing low (for shorts) is my first confirmation.

**✅ Confirmation 2 — Return to Order Block:** Price should retrace into the order block I marked during Step 2. This is the optimal entry point, not the breakout candle itself.

**✅ Confirmation 3 — FVG Within the Order Block:** If there's a fair value gap nested inside or near the order block, that's my refined entry. I'll target the 50% of the FVG as my entry price.

**✅ Confirmation 4 — Time of Day:** Is this happening inside a kill zone? The New York AM kill zone (7 AM – 10 AM ET) is where the highest-probability post-data moves occur. If I'm trying to enter at noon with no institutional session overlap, I'm forcing it.

**✅ Confirmation 5 — Stop Placement:** My stop goes below the order block (for longs) or above it (for shorts) — not a fixed pip stop. This respects the structure and avoids getting hunted on normal retracement.

This framework works across pairs — DXY-correlated pairs like EURUSD, GBPUSD, and USDJPY are the most reactive to Retail Sales data, but the same ICT logic applies to NAS100 and US30 around USD data releases.

## Building Your Retail Sales Week Game Plan

To summarize the full pre-to-post ICT framework:

1. **Sunday/Monday:** Identify the weekly draw on liquidity. Mark order blocks and FVGs on the 4H and Daily.
2. **Night before data:** Mark the Asian session range. Note which side has more resting liquidity.
3. **Early London:** Watch for a displacement sweep of the Asian range. This often telegraphs direction.
4. **Data release (8:30 ET):** Expect the spike and sweep. Do NOT enter on the spike.
5. **Post-data:** Wait for the MSS, return to the order block, confirm the FVG, check kill zone timing, then execute with structural stop placement.

This isn't a system that requires you to predict the number. It's a system that reads *how price behaves around the number* — which is far more consistent and far more learnable.
